Auto-AmbuBag - A Portable Automated BVM

Collaboration with Indus Hospital for use in disaster response and rural healthcare in Pakistan.

From Clinical Need to Working Device

The Challenge: Requirements from Indus Hospital

The **Indus Hospital & Health Network** is the largest health network in Pakistan providing absolutely free healthcare to 6 million people annually. As Pakistan is among the top 5 climate change vulnerable nations, devastated by frequent monsoon floods, the hospital desires a **low-cost, safe, and portable ventilator** to support health needs during disasters and in hard-to-reach rural areas. Key requirements included:

  • **Portability:** Light weight and small size.
  • **Reliability:** Can operate on **battery backup for at least 2 hours** to deal with unreliable power.
  • **Transport Ready:** Can be used for transporting patients over unpaved or damaged roads.
  • **Safety:** Features for lung pressure monitoring and emergency stop.
  • **Control:** User controls for breath per minute (BPM), Tidal Volume, and Inhale-Exhale (I/E) ratio with specific ranges.
  • **Target User:** Device must work for adults (no infant or child support in V1).
  • **Cost Constraint:** Keep total cost under **$1000**.

Our Solution: Building on Open-Source Foundations

To address this challenge, we started with the **MIT Emergency Ventilator** open-source project, conceived during the COVID-19 ventilator shortage. We researched projects that built on the MIT design and found the **MPS design** to be a great fit. Our solution learns from both these projects, modifying them significantly to meet the specific requirements of the Auto-AmbuBag for the Indus Hospital context.

Our Design Process: A Quick Overview

Starting from the requirements, we held a series of meetings with our advisors to explore hardware and software options. Given the groundwork laid by the MIT & MPS prototypes, we made several foundational decisions quickly:

  • Using an **Arduino** as the microcontroller.
  • Employing a system of **gears for rotational force** over linear actuators, resulting in a smaller device and better duty cycle.
  • Utilizing an **encoder on the motor** for granular, closed-loop control of respiration phases, a key learning from the MIT project.
  • Implementing the recommended user interface controls for BPM, Tidal Volume, I/E ratio, and pressure.

While leveraging existing state-of-the-art knowledge allowed for rapid progress, the unique Indus Hospital use case necessitated innovation and deviation from the reference designs. As a result, we:

  • **Reduced the physical size** by finding smaller components (smaller power supply, integrated motor gears), making the **Auto-AmbuBag significantly smaller** than both the MIT and MPS devices.
  • Integrated a **power management and battery charging module** to handle power outages seamlessly.
  • Switched to a **12V ecosystem** to allow charging and operation directly from an ambulance's power outlet during patient transport.
  • **Conserved power** by using a lower-torque **9 N-m motor** (sufficient for the non-COVID patient use case) and selecting a **non-touch LCD display** with aggressive brightness controls, which also eliminated the need for any active cooling.
  • Added an **SpO2 sensor** to monitor the patient’s oxygen saturation, providing critical, real-time data to the clinician on the device screen.

These modifications balanced the advantages of open-source knowledge with the need to meet specific, real-world requirements.

Entry 1: Power System

Challenge:

To design a power system that could run continuously for 4+ hours when plugged in and **2+ hours on battery**.

Options Explored:
  • We considered a 24V motor for efficiency but chose a **12V motor** to avoid introducing a boost converter and a potential point of failure.
  • We selected a **low-power, 2.4-inch LCD display** with configurable backlight. Aggressive backlight control reduced its consumption to a negligible 0.05Ah.
  • Under typical configuration used by Indus (15 bpm, Tidal vol of 750), the device drew **less than 1 A of power hourly** while operating the BVM (tested locally using latex balloons to simulate lung resistance).
Decision & Rationale:

We selected a **4-cell and 6Ah Lithium-Ion battery** that is an off-the-shelf component, easily procured and replaceable in Pakistan. Its modularity allows us to double the capacity to 12Ah within the same design by adding one more battery. We are confident this comfortably **exceeds the 2-hour battery operation time requirement** for the Auto-AmbuBag.

For future iterations, we are considering a different, longer-cycle life battery (8Ah, 4000+ cycles, $18) as a potential cost and reliability improvement.

Entry 2: The Motor - The Heart of the system

Challenge:

To generate **controlled, high torque** to squeeze the AmbuBag while operating under low power and during transport. This requires a high torque, low revolutions per minute (rpm) motor.

Options Explored:
  • We struggled first with a **high-rpm brushless DC motor** (4000 rpm) which required a bulky, complex gear system, making the device too large.
  • We then spent over a month struggling with multiple **low-rpm stepper motors** and various drivers (TMC2208, TB6600, BTS7960). The stepper motors consistently **skipped under load** and were deemed fundamentally unsuitable for this closed-loop control application by the manufacturer.
  • After returning to DC motors, a **TorqueNADO MAX (5 N-m)** motor was too weak at higher respiration rates.
  • We finally selected a **goBILDA Yellow Jacket DC motor with 9 N-m torque at 84 rpm speed**. This motor worked perfectly and had a built-in encoder.
Decision & Rationale:

We chose the **goBILDA Yellow Jacket DC brushed motor**. This motor met all our needs: **low rpm, high torque, encoder support for closed-loop operation, and low cost ($55)**. Its ecosystem simplified the mechanical design, providing a stable and reliable "heart" for the system after a challenging development phase.

goBILDA Yellow Jacket motor with encoder

Entry 3: User Interface (UI) & Controls

Challenge:

To create a simple, intuitive, and **power-efficient** interface for clinicians to view and precisely set ventilation parameters (BPM, Tidal Volume, I/E Ratio, Pressure, SpO2) as specified by our Indus Hospital partners.

Options Explored:
  • **Large Touch Screen:** Rejected due to high cost, complexity, and being **too power-hungry** for the mobile battery requirement.
  • **Multiple Potentiometers:** Rejected due to being imprecise and lacking the necessary digital numerical feedback for clinical use.
  • **LCD Screen with Rotary Encoder:** This option offered the ideal balance of **low power consumption, cost-effectiveness, and precise digital input** via a menu system.
Decision & Rationale:

We selected a **16x2 LCD screen with a single rotary encoder and push-button**. This interface was the most robust and cost-effective solution. It allowed me to program a simple, menu-driven system where clinicians could **precisely set and confirm each parameter**, directly addressing the hospital's exact usability and power constraints.

From a simple potentiometer to the final LCD and encoder interface.

Real-World Application & Pilot with Indus Hospital

Collaboration with Indus Hospital & Health Network

This project has been a deep collaboration with Indus Hospital staff from the start. The current design meets all required specifications and has been **approved by the biomedical department at Indus**. Most of the original requirements are successfully implemented and tested. To ready the device for crucial field testing in Pakistan, the following essential work remains to be completed:

Remaining Work: Pressure Sensor Integration

We must integrate a pressure sensor to continuously monitor and control the pressure in the patient's airways. While the device will be field tested initially at lower settings, this is a paramount safety feature. We plan to use the medical-grade **Honeywell pressure sensor** (HSCMRRV001PD2A3) for this task.
