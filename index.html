Auto-AmbuBag - A Portable Automated BVM

Collaboration with Indus Hospital for use in disaster response and rural healthcare in Pakistan.

From Clinical Need to Working Device

The Challenge: Requirements from Indus Hospital

The **Indus Hospital & Health Network** is the largest health network in Pakistan, providing absolutely free healthcare to 6 million people annually. As Pakistan is among the top 5 climate change vulnerable nations, devastated by frequent monsoon floods, the hospital desires a **low-cost, safe, and portable ventilator** to support health needs during disasters and in hard-to-reach rural areas. The key requirements were:

  • **Portability:** Light weight and small size.
  • **Reliability:** Can operate on **battery backup for at least 2 hours** to deal with unreliable or damaged power infrastructure.
  • **Transport Ready:** Can be used for transporting patients over unpaved or damaged roads.
  • **Safety:** Features for lung pressure monitoring and emergency stop.
  • **Control:** User controls for breath per minute (BPM), Tidal Volume, and Inhale-Exhale (I/E) ratio with specific ranges.
  • **Target User:** Device needs to work only for adults (no infant or child support in V1).
  • **Cost Constraint:** Keep total cost under **$1000** (retail cost is typically over $10,000).

Our Solution: Building on Open-Source Foundations

To solve this problem, we started with the **MIT Emergency Ventilator** open-source project. The E-Vent project was conceived at the height of COVID-19, when a worldwide shortage of ventilators was causing severe challenges. We researched several other open-source projects built on the MIT design and found the **MPS design** to be a good fit. Our solution learns from both these projects and modifies them in several key ways to meet the requirements for the Auto-AmbuBag.

Our Design Process: A Quick Overview

Starting from the requirements, we held a series of meetings with our advisors who suggested various options to explore for hardware and software needs. Given the trail blazed by MIT and MPS prototypes, some decisions were relatively easy to make, leading to rapid initial progress:

  • Using an **Arduino** as the microcontroller for the AutoBVM.
  • Employing a system of **gears for rotational force** to achieve a smaller device and better duty cycle.
  • Utilizing an **encoder on the motor** for granular, closed-loop control of respiration phases, a key learning from the MIT project.
  • Implementing the standard user interface controls for BPM, Tidal Volume, I/E ratio, and lung pressure.

There were, however, unique requirements for the Indus Hospital use case that necessitated innovation and deviation from the original design. As a result, we:

  • **Reduced the physical size** significantly by making efficient use of space and finding smaller components (smaller power supply, integrated gears). The Auto-AmbuBag device is significantly smaller than both MIT and MPS devices.
  • Incorporated a **power management and battery charging module** to enable switching over to the battery in case of power outages.
  • Switched to a **12V ecosystem** to enable charging and operating the device from the power outlet of an ambulance during patient transportation.
  • **Conserved power** by using a **9 N-m motor** (lower power than the 15 N-m minimum required for COVID patients) and selecting a **non-touch screen display** with aggressive brightness controls, which also eliminated the need for active cooling.
  • Added an **SpO2 sensor** to monitor oxygen saturation, displaying the measurement on the device screen for the clinician.

Entry 1: Power System

Battery used in the design

Challenge:

To design a power system that could run continuously for 4+ hours when plugged in and **2+ hours on battery**.

Options Explored:
  • A 24V motor was considered for efficiency but rejected as it would require a boost converter, introducing complexity and a potential failure point. We found that **12V motors** could efficiently manage the required load.
  • We selected a low-power, **2.4-inch LCD display** with aggressive backlight control, reducing its power consumption to approximately **0.05Ah**, which is negligible.
  • Under typical configuration used by Indus, the device operated the BVM while drawing **less than 1 A of power hourly** (tested locally using latex balloons to simulate lung resistance).
Decision & Rationale:

We picked an off-the-shelf **4-cell and 6Ah Lithium-Ion battery** that is easily procured and replaceable in Pakistan. We can double the total capacity to 12Ah within the same design by adding one more battery, comfortably exceeding the 2-hour battery operation time requirement. For future iterations, we are considering a different battery (8Ah, longer cycle life, half the price) for potential cost and reliability improvements.

Entry 2: The Motor - The Heart of the system

goBILDA Yellow Jacket motor with encoder

Challenge:

To generate **controlled, high torque** to squeeze the AmbuBag while operating in limited power conditions or during transport. This requires a high torque, low revolutions per minute (rpm) motor.

Options Explored:
  • A donated, high-rpm brushless DC motor required a complex **100:1 planetary gear** system, resulting in excessive device size.
  • We spent over a month trying multiple low-rpm **stepper motors** and drivers (TMC2208, TB6600, BTS7960). The stepper motors consistently **skipped under load**, even with an encoder, and were deemed fundamentally unsuitable by the manufacturer.
  • A **TorqueNADO MAX (5 N-m)** DC motor lacked the necessary torque at higher BPMs/tidal volumes.
  • We finally selected a **goBILDA Yellow Jacket DC motor with 9 N-m torque at 84 rpm speed**, which performed perfectly and included built-in encoder support.
Decision & Rationale:

We picked the **goBILDA Yellow Jacket DC brushed motor**. This motor met all our needs: **low rpm, high torque, encoder support for closed-loop operation, and low cost ($55)**. The compatibility of the goBILDA ecosystem provided readily available mounts and drivers, resulting in a stable and reliable component for the system.

Entry 3: User Interface (UI) & Controls

From a simple potentiometer to the final LCD and encoder interface.

Challenge:

To create a simple, intuitive, and **power-efficient** interface for clinicians to view and precisely set ventilation parameters (BPM, Tidal Volume, I/E Ratio, Pressure, User messages).

Options Explored:
  • **Large Touch Screen:** Rejected due to high cost and **excessive power consumption** for a battery-operated device.
  • **Multiple Potentiometers:** Rejected due to being imprecise and lacking the necessary digital numerical feedback for clinical use.
  • **LCD Screen with Rotary Encoder:** This option offered the ideal balance of **cost, low power, and precise digital control** within a simple menu system.
Decision & Rationale:

We selected a **16x2 LCD screen with a single rotary encoder and push-button**. This interface was the most robust and cost-effective, allowing for a programmed menu where clinicians could **precisely set and confirm** each parameter, meeting the hospital's exact usability and precision requirements.

Real-World Application & Pilot with Indus Hospital

Collaboration with Indus Hospital & Health Network

This project has been a deep collaboration with Indus Hospital staff from the start. The design meets all required specifications and has been **approved by the biomedical department at Indus**. Most of the original requirements are successfully implemented and tested. To ready the device for crucial field testing in Pakistan, the following essential work remains to be completed:

Remaining Work: Pressure Sensor Integration

We must integrate a pressure sensor to continuously monitor and control the pressure in the patient's airways. While this device will be field tested initially at lower settings, this is a paramount safety feature. We will be using the medical-grade **Honeywell pressure sensor** (HSCMRRV001PD2A3) for this task.
