Auto-AmbuBag - A Portable Automated BVM

Collaboration with Indus Hospital for use in disaster response and rural healthcare in Pakistan.

From Clinical Need to Working Device

The Challenge: Requirements from Indus Hospital

Indus Hospital & Health Network is the largest health network in Pakistan providing absolutely free healthcare to 6 million people annually. Pakistan is also among the top 5 climate change vulnerable nations, devastated by frequent monsoon floods. To better support health needs during disasters and hard-to-reach rural areas, Indus Hospital desires to create a **low-cost ventilator that is safe and portable**. The key requirements were:

  • **Light weight and small size** for portability.
  • Can operate on **battery backup for at least 2 hours** to deal with unreliable or damaged power infrastructure.
  • Can be used for **transporting patients** over unpaved or damaged roads.
  • **Safety features** for lung pressure monitoring and emergency stop.
  • **User controls** for breath per minute, Tidal Volume, and Inhale-Exhale ratio with specific ranges.
  • In the first version, the device needs to work only for **adults** (no infant or child support).
  • Keep **cost under $1000** (retail cost is typically over $10,000).

Our Solution: Building on Open-Source Foundations

To solve this problem, we started from the **MIT Emergency Ventilator** open-source project, conceived during the COVID-19 ventilator shortage. We researched several other open-source projects that build on the MIT design and found the **MPS design** to be a good fit. Our solution learns from both these projects and modifies them in several key ways to meet the unique requirements for the **Auto-AmbuBag**.

Our Design Process: A Quick Overview

Starting from the requirements, we held a series of meetings with our Advisors who suggested various options to explore for hardware and software needs. Given the trail blazed by MIT & MPS prototypes, some decisions were relatively easy to make:

  • Using an **Arduino** as the microcontroller for the AutoBVM.
  • Using a system of **gears to exert rotational force**, rather than linear force with actuators, for a smaller device and better reliability/duty cycle.
  • Utilizing an **encoder on the motor** for granular control of respiration phases, a key learning from the MIT project.
  • Implementing the same user interface recommendation made by the MIT project (user controls for BPM, Tidal Volume, I/E ratio and lung pressure).

By leveraging existing state-of-the-art knowledge, we made rapid progress. However, requirements unique to the Indus Hospital use case necessitated innovation and deviation from the original design. As a result, we:

  • **Reduced the physical size** significantly by finding smaller components (power supply, integrated gears) and making efficient use of internal space.
  • Incorporated a **power management and battery charging module** to handle power outages.
  • Switched to a **12V ecosystem** to enable charging and operating from an ambulance's power outlet.
  • **Conserved power** by using a 9 N-m motor instead of a higher-powered (15 N-m) one, selecting a **non-touch LCD display** with aggressive brightness controls, and eliminating the need for active cooling due to low heat production.
  • Added an **SpO2 sensor** to monitor oxygen saturation, allowing clinicians to adjust ventilator settings based on patient vitals displayed on the screen.

The modifications above made it possible to take advantage of existing open-source knowledge where possible, while adjusting the design for the unique needs we had to meet.

Entry 1: Power System

Challenge:

To design a power system that could run continuously for 4+ hours when plugged in and **2+ hours on battery**.

Options Explored:
  • We avoided a more efficient **24V motor** that would require a complex boost converter, opting instead for a simpler **12V motor** that managed the required load efficiently.
  • We selected a low-power, **2.4 inch LCD display** with configurable backlight. Aggressive backlight control reduced consumption to a negligible 0.05Ah.
  • In the typical Indus Hospital configuration (15 bpm, 750 tidal volume), the device drew **less than 1 A of power hourly**, tested locally using latex balloons to simulate lung resistance.
Decision & Rationale:

We picked a **4-cell and 6Ah Lithium-Ion battery**. This is an off-the-shelf component, easily procured and replaceable in Pakistan. We are confident this comfortably **exceeds the 2 hour battery operation time**. The design allows for doubling the capacity to 12Ah with a second battery if future testing demands it, without altering the device's size.

Entry 2: The Motor - The Heart of the system

Challenge:

To generate **controlled, high torque** to squeeze the AmbuBag while operating under low power and during patient transport.

Options Explored:
  • We first struggled with a **donated high-rpm brushless DC motor** (4000 rpm), which required a bulky, complex gear system (100:1 planetary gear) that made the device too large.
  • We then spent a month on **low-rpm stepper motors** and various drivers (TMC2208, TB6600, BTS7960). The stepper motor constantly **skipped under load** and was stubbornly difficult to control, even with an encoder. We concluded they are fundamentally unsuitable for this use case.
  • We switched back to DC motors, first trying a **TorqueNADO MAX (5 N-m)**, which was smooth but lacked the necessary torque at higher respiration rates.
  • We finally selected a **goBILDA Yellow Jacket DC motor with 9 N-m torque at 84 rpm speed**. This motor has built-in encoder support and worked perfectly across all required configuration ranges.
Decision & Rationale:

We picked the **goBILDA Yellow Jacket DC brushed motor**. This motor met all our non-negotiable needs: **low rpm, high torque, encoder support for closed-loop operation**, and a low cost of $55. The supporting goBILDA ecosystem provided easily available mounts and drivers, accelerating our progress after a frustrating start. This was the most critical component decision.

Entry 3: User Interface (UI) & Controls

Challenge:

To create a simple, intuitive, and **power-efficient** interface for clinicians to view and set ventilation parameters (BPM, Tidal Volume, I/E Ratio, Pressure, SpO2) as specified by our Indus Hospital partners.

Options Explored:
  • **Large Touch Screen:** Intuitive and flexible, but was rejected early due to high cost and **excessive power consumption**. We settled for a small 2.4-inch LCD digital display for simple readouts.
  • **Multiple Potentiometers:** Simple hardware, but they are imprecise and lacked necessary numerical feedback for exact clinical settings.
  • **LCD Screen with Rotary Encoder:** This option offered a balance of **cost, low power, and precise digital control** within a simple menu-driven system.
Decision & Rationale:

We selected a **16x2 LCD screen with a single rotary encoder and push-button**. This interface proved the most robust and cost-effective solution. The rotary encoder allowed me to program a simple menu structure where clinicians could **precisely set and confirm** each parameter digitally, meeting the hospital's exact usability and precision requirements.

Real-World Application & Pilot with Indus Hospital

Collaboration with Indus Hospital & Health Network

This project has been a deep collaboration with Indus Hospital staff from the start. The current design meets all original specifications and has been **approved by the biomedical department at Indus**. Most of the requirements are successfully implemented and tested. To ready the device for field testing at Indus Hospital, the following critical safety feature remains to be completed:

Remaining Work: Pressure Sensor Integration

We need a pressure sensor to continuously monitor and control the pressure in the patient's airways. While this device will be field tested at lower settings, this is an important safety feature we plan to add to the Auto-AmbuBag first. We will be using the **Honeywell medical grade pressure sensor** (HSCMRRV001PD2A3) for this task.
