Auto-AmbuBag - A Portable Automated BVM

Collaboration with Indus Hospital for use in disaster response and rural healthcare in Pakistan.

From Clinical Need to Working Device

The Challenge: Requirements from Indus Hospital

The **Indus Hospital & Health Network** is the largest health network in Pakistan, providing absolutely free healthcare to 6 million people annually. Pakistan is also among the top 5 climate change vulnerable nations, devastated by frequent monsoon floods. To better support health needs during disasters and in hard-to-reach rural areas, the hospital desires a **low-cost, safe, and portable ventilator**. The key requirements were:

  • **Portability:** Light weight and small size.
  • **Reliability:** Can operate on **battery backup for at least 2 hours** to deal with unreliable or damaged power infrastructure.
  • **Transport Ready:** Can be used for transporting patients over unpaved or damaged roads.
  • **Safety:** Features for lung pressure monitoring and emergency stop.
  • **Control:** User controls for breath per minute (BPM), Tidal Volume, and Inhale-Exhale (I/E) ratio with specific ranges.
  • **Target User:** Device needs to work only for adults (no infant or child support in V1).
  • **Cost Constraint:** Keep total cost under **$1000** (retail cost is typically over $10,000).

Our Solution: Building on Open-Source Foundations

To solve this problem, we started with the **MIT Emergency Ventilator** open-source project, conceived during the COVID-19 ventilator shortage. We researched several other projects built on the MIT design and found the **MPS design** to be a good fit. Our solution learns from both these projects and modifies them in several key ways to meet the specific requirements for the Auto-AmbuBag.

Our Design Process: A Quick Overview

Starting from the requirements, we held a series of meetings with our Advisors to explore hardware and software options. Given the trail blazed by MIT and MPS prototypes, some decisions were relatively easy to make:

  • Using an **Arduino** as the microcontroller for the AutoBVM.
  • Using a system of **gears to exert rotational force**, rather than linear force with actuators. This resulted in a smaller device for portability and better duty cycle.
  • Utilizing an **encoder on the motor** to granularly control the speed and range of motion of the respiration phases, a key learning from the MIT project.
  • Implementing the same user interface recommendation made by the MIT project (user controls for BPM, Tidal Volume, I/E ratio and lung pressure).

By leveraging existing state-of-the-art knowledge, we made rapid progress. However, requirements unique to the Indus Hospital use case necessitated innovation and deviation from the original design. As a result, we:

  • **Reduced the physical size** by making efficient use of space and finding smaller components (power supply, integrated gears). The Auto-AmbuBag device is significantly smaller than both MIT and MPS devices.
  • Incorporated a **power management and battery charging module** to enable seamless switching over to the battery during power outages.
  • Switched to a **12V ecosystem** to enable charging and operating the device from the power outlet of an ambulance during patient transportation.
  • **Conserved power** by using a **9 N-m motor** (sufficient for this use case, unlike the 15 N-m required for COVID patients), utilizing a **non-touch screen display** with aggressive brightness controls, and eliminating the need for active cooling.
  • Added an **SpO2 sensor** to monitor oxygen saturation, displaying the measurement on the screen for the clinician to modify ventilator settings as needed.

The modifications above made it possible to take advantage of existing open-source knowledge where possible, while adjusting the design for the unique needs we had to meet.

Entry 1: Power System

Battery used in the design

Challenge:

To design a power system that could run continuously for 4+ hours when plugged in and **2+ hours on battery**.

Options Explored:
  • We avoided a more efficient 24V motor to prevent adding a boost converter and a potential failure point, finding that **12V motors** could manage the required load.
  • The second most power-hungry component, the display screen, was a low-power, 2.4-inch LCD display. With aggressive backlight control, we reduced its consumption to approximately **0.05Ah, which is negligible**.
  • In typical configuration (15 bpm, 750 tidal volume), the device drew **less than 1 A of power hourly** (tested locally using latex balloons to simulate lung resistance).
Decision & Rationale:

We picked a **4-cell and 6Ah Lithium-Ion battery** that is an off-the-shelf component, easily procured and replaceable in Pakistan. Its capacity ensures we will **comfortably exceed the 2-hour battery operation time**. The design can be easily upgraded to 12Ah capacity by adding one more battery, increasing weight by 529g and cost by ~$40, while maintaining the size.

For future iterations, we are considering a different battery (8Ah, longer cycle life, half the price) as a potential improvement to experiment with.

Entry 2: The Motor - The Heart of the system

goBILDA Yellow Jacket motor with encoder

Challenge:

To generate **controlled torque** to squeeze the AmbuBag while operating in limited power conditions or during transport. This requires a high torque, low revolutions per minute (rpm) motor.

Options Explored:
  • We first tried a donated, high-rpm brushless DC motor, but required a complex **100:1 planetary gear** system that made the device too big.
  • We next switched to low-rpm **stepper motors** and multiple drivers (TMC2208, TB6600, BTS7960). The stepper motors consistently **skipped under load** and were deemed fundamentally unsuitable for this use case by the manufacturer, even with an encoder.
  • After returning to DC motors, a **TorqueNADO MAX (5 N-m)** motor lacked the necessary torque at higher BPMs.
  • We finally selected a **goBILDA Yellow Jacket DC motor with 9 N-m torque at 84 rpm speed**. This motor worked perfectly and has built-in encoder support.
Decision & Rationale:

We picked the **goBILDA Yellow Jacket DC brushed motor**. This motor met all our needs: **low rpm, high torque, encoder support for closed-loop operation, and low cost ($55)**. The goBILDA ecosystem also provided easily available mounts and drivers, accelerating our progress and resulting in a robust choice for the system's most critical component.

Entry 3: User Interface (UI) & Controls

From a simple potentiometer to the final LCD and encoder interface.

Challenge:

To create a simple, intuitive, and **power-efficient** interface for clinicians to view and precisely set ventilation parameters (BPM, Tidal Volume, I/E Ratio, Pressure, User messages) as specified by our Indus Hospital partners.

Options Explored:
  • **Large Touch Screen:** Rejected due to high cost and **excessive power consumption**. We settled for a small 2.4-inch LCD digital display for simple readouts.
  • **Multiple Potentiometers:** Rejected due to being imprecise and lacking the necessary digital numerical feedback for exact clinical settings.
  • **LCD Screen with Rotary Encoder:** This option offered a balance of **cost, low power, and precise digital control** within a simple menu-driven system.
Decision & Rationale:

We selected a **16x2 LCD screen with a single rotary encoder and push-button**. This interface was the most robust and cost-effective. It allowed me to program a simple menu structure where clinicians could **precisely set and confirm** each parameter digitally, meeting the hospital's exact usability and precision requirements.

Real-World Application & Pilot with Indus Hospital

Collaboration with Indus Hospital & Health Network

This project has been a deep collaboration with Indus Hospital staff from the start. The design meets all required specifications and has been **approved by the biomedical department at Indus**. Most of the original requirements are successfully implemented and tested. To ready the device for field testing at Indus Hospital, the following essential work remains to be completed:

Remaining Work: Pressure Sensor Integration

We must integrate a pressure sensor to continuously monitor and control the pressure in the patient's airways. While this device will be field tested at lower settings, this is a paramount safety feature. We will be using the medical-grade **Honeywell pressure sensor** (HSCMRRV001PD2A3) for this task.
